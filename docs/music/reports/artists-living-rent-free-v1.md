# Artists Living Rent-Free v1

Generated: 2026-06-12

## Question

Which artists consume meaningful listening time without earning a clearly defined place in my musical story?

This report is not about disliked artists.

It is about artists who repeatedly occupy listening space while exhibiting weak attachment, high skip behavior, limited catalog engagement, or unresolved relationship status.

---

# Executive Summary

Several artists emerged as strong candidates for "living rent-free" in the listening history.

The most compelling examples are not artists with low play counts.

They are artists that received repeated opportunities over many years but never developed into Identity, Canon, Personal, Awe, or Complicated relationships.

Key findings:

* The Who appears to be a repeated catalog exploration that never fully took hold.
* Foreigner has surprisingly high exposure and high skip behavior across eleven years.
* Could be the same for artists like John Denver, Elton John, Eric Clapton, Steely Dan
* The Rolling Stones may represent the largest unresolved relationship discovered so far.
* One-Song Residents emerged as a distinct phenomenon separate from rent-free artists.
* Historical Artifacts appear to represent former listening chapters rather than failed relationships.

# Failed Relationships

Metrics

Observation

Prior discussion suggested that only a handful of Who songs remain meaningful.

## Foreigner

Metrics

* Events: 163
* Skip Rate: 65.0%
* Distinct Tracks: 33
* Active Years: 11

Observation

Foreigner is one of the strongest rent-free candidates discovered in the first report.

The catalog has been sampled broadly and consistently across more than a decade, yet nearly two-thirds of encounters result in skips.

This suggests familiarity without attachment.

## The Rolling Stones

Metrics

* Events: 476
* Skip Rate: 59.2%
* Distinct Tracks: 166
* Active Years: 11

Observation

This may be the most surprising result in the report.

The amount of catalog exposure is enormous.

The data suggests repeated attempts to engage with the catalog over many years.

Despite that exposure, the Stones have not earned a clear relationship role.

Open Question:

Why do the Stones occupy so much listening space without occupying comparable emotional space?
